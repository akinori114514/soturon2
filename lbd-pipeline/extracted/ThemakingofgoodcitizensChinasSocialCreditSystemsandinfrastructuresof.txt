INTRODUCTION

China is currently witnessing rapid progress in deploying the Social Credit Systems (SCSs).
The central government has updated institutional structures and data platforms for gathering
credit data and offering rewards and sanctions (Creemers, 2018). Municipal governments
are progressively conducting multiple local schemes to rate personal social credit
(Liu, 2019). Meanwhile, technology companies have also launched digital platforms for

evaluating customer credit (Chong, 2019). While commercial systems are voluntary and
often emphasize lending and ﬁnancial activities, government‐led SCSs are mandatory and
claim the fundamental goal is to enhance the sincerity and civic virtue, and enforce law‐
abiding behaviors (Creemers, 2018; Zhang, 2020). Scholars have argued that the emerging
SCSs entail China's transformation into a data‐driven society (Backer, 2019; Liang
et al., 2018).
    Although China's national SCSs have attracted academic and public attention, hardly
any work has addressed the implementation of SCSs at local levels where individual scoring
systems are being tested. In fact, multiple cities have carried out the SCSs to rate and rank
social credit of citizens and the credit scores further determine whether people receive
beneﬁts or restrictions in these cities. These local initiatives show the trajectory towards an
upgrading system for social management and law enforcement in China (Creemers, 2018).
At the same time, local SCSs can be situated in the increasingly prevalent practice of social
quantiﬁcation throughout the world (Cheney‐Lippold, 2018; Couldry & Mejias, 2019; Mac
Síthigh & Siems, 2019).
    Drawing on policy documentation, ofﬁcial regulations and platforms, and news coverage,
and building on critical studies of quantiﬁcation, we consider China's municipal SCSs as
social quantiﬁcation infrastructures and investigate their technological structures, political
logic, and potential implications. By social quantiﬁcation infrastructures, we refer to an
assemblage of institutional, regulatory, material, and social layers of data relations between
governments and citizens developed to monitor and regulate people in increasingly dataﬁed
ways. Our ﬁndings suggest that local governments follow the same logic and procedure to
deploy the SCSs as standardized processes to quantify various dimensions of personal life
into a common metric, whereas potential indicators and scoring mechanisms are
substantially ﬂexible across cities. We argue that the SCSs strengthen the state's
management of citizens in substantively important ways, speciﬁcally by tightly mingling
quantiﬁcation practices with social and economic life of citizens. People are hence rendered
measurable, comparable, and governable through increasing social quantiﬁcation of
everyday life.


Quantiﬁcation as a form of social control
Data have long been a key source of power and legitimacy. The collection of population data
and the use of numbers have always been central to states and public sectors, reﬂecting the
rise of bureaucracy and the rationalization of social control (Bouk, 2015; Desrosieres, 1998;
Foucault, 1978; Scott, 1998). For centuries, governments have built data infrastructures for
various purposes such as crime control, citizenship identiﬁcation, and surveillance
(Lauer, 2017; Lyon, 2007). Through quantiﬁcation, state power and other actors transform
social structures into legal and bureaucratic categories (Bouk, 2015).
    Notably, the process of quantiﬁcation is not natural; instead, it is inherent in technological
systems and socially constructed, aiming to reduce social and political complexities (Bowker
& Star, 2000; Scott, 1998). Social quantiﬁcation thus further promotes the legibility and
simpliﬁcation of the society and makes people calculable and governable (Scott, 1998). In
doing so, quantiﬁcation subsequently determines who should be targeted for access,
eligibility, suspicion, or exclusion. Thus, social quantiﬁcation generates ways of seeing and
knowing people and is part of the social infrastructures that regulate and steer individual and
organizational behaviors (Bowker & Star, 2000; Espeland & Sauder, 2007).
    There are at least three waves of social quantiﬁcation. Numbers and statistics were used
by European nations to categorize the population in the nineteenth century
(Desrosieres, 1998; Scott, 1998). The early practices were led primarily by governments

and public sectors, attempting to count people and further generate knowledge about
society. The abstraction of social life also enabled a new form of social control by creating
legible people and managing life (Scott, 1998). During the late nineteenth century, consumer
credit rating was initially developed in the United States to evaluate the creditworthiness of
individuals and ﬁrms (Lauer, 2017). In the early 20th century, the rise of welfare states
further enhanced quantiﬁcation practices like census and public statistics in Europe and the
United States (Lips et al., 2009). Although automated tools like punch‐card machines had
been used during the period, early quantiﬁcation relied heavily on paper‐based administra-
tion and human assessment.
    The second wave of development took place in the 1960s when credit rating agencies
progressively adopted relational databases and information infrastructures for computeriz-
ing ﬁnancial credit reporting (Lauer, 2017). It was also facilitated by the so‐called social
indicators movement aiming to measure social values using numbers and data. Compared
to the ﬁrst wave, private corporations and markets played increasingly important roles in
transforming social life into metrics and data (Citron & Pasquale, 2014). Credit scoring
systems (e.g., FICO) and constant surveillance were developed by private sectors for
evaluating whether people would default on their debts and for building “the scored society”
(Citron & Pasquale, 2014; Fourcade & Healy, 2017). While the ﬁrst wave quantiﬁcation
aimed to count people at the population level, the second wave focused on creating proﬁling
and measurement at the individual level.
    Big data and computational revolutions promote third‐wave development by offering
automated decision‐making and massive data entrenched in institutional infrastructures and
digital platforms (Couldry & Mejias, 2019; Livingstone, 2019; Pasquale, 2015). Massive
databases are aggregated and explored for identifying patterns and correlations that were
previously impossible (Beer, 2018; boyd & Crawford, 2012). For instance, credit reporting
agencies have expanded scoring systems by gathering data relating to nonﬁnancial
payments, digital footprints, and psychometric testing (Aitken, 2017). Media industries also
adopt digital tools to improve audience analytics (Livingstone, 2019). Moreover, technology
companies now can predict individual behaviors via predictive algorithms (Brayne, 2017;
Dencik et al., 2019). As a result, people are constantly assessed in the context of statistical
estimation, and further transformed into automation and statistical analytics (Cheney‐
Lippold, 2018; Couldry & Mejias, 2019).
    While private sectors largely dominate the deployment of digital technologies in social
quantiﬁcation (Beer, 2018; Couldry & Mejias, 2019), state actors are vigorously adopting
new technologies and building data infrastructures (Dencik et al., 2019). India, for instance,
has launched a biometric identiﬁcation database named Aadhaar for providing public
services and evaluating citizens (Singh, 2020). Law enforcement in the US employs big data
analytics to calculate risk scores and transform surveillance practices (Brayne, 2017).
Further, the EU has proposed iBorderCtrl project to detect deception and strengthen border
control using emotional AI (Sánchez‐Monedero & Dencik, 2020). Obviously, current social
quantiﬁcation focuses on automated technologies and data warehouses to real‐time
evaluate and monitor individuals.
    To be sure, the People's Republic of China (PRC) has practiced social quantiﬁcation to
assess and classify people (Ghosh, 2020). Dang'an (personnel dossiers), for instance, is
used to aggregate personal information like education and employment. Another system
Hukou (household registration) can determine whether people have access to housing,
healthcare, and education in cities. More recently, China has aggressively employed digital
technologies to monitor and categorize citizens. For instance, local authorities have
launched “citizen morality ﬁles” for recording and assessing neighbors’ good deeds
(Jiang, 2020). In 2020, the Chinese governments have collaborated with tech giants Alibaba
and Tencent to prevent the spread of COVID‐19 by quantifying people's travel history and

health status (Liang, 2020). Ethnic sorting, drones, and GPS tracker have been used to
strengthen surveillance in Xinjiang (Leibold, 2020). The development of these initiatives
suggests that China has been heavily investing in leveraging digital technologies for social
quantiﬁcation and social management.


SCSs in China
China's credit system was initially constructed for the socialist market economy in the early
1990s (Creemers, 2018). Similar to other consumer credit systems such as FICO, the
original SCSs emphasized evaluating the creditworthiness of individuals (Jiang, 2020). Yet,
it has gradually grown to include not only individuals and corporates but also organizations
and governmental entities since 2002 (Creemers, 2018; Liang et al., 2018). In 2007, China's
State Council regarded social credit as a key component of China's market economy. In
2014, the State Council further improved the deployment of national SCSs. The current
SCSs involve both ﬁnancial and moralistic trustworthiness, attempting to evaluate society,
government, judiciary, and commerce. It will eventually promote sincerity and social morality
and meantime enforce legal compliance (Zhang, 2020).
     To achieve the goal, national SCSs are championed by updated institutional structures
and data infrastructures (e.g., Credit China). These deployments enable national SCSs to
collect and extract large amounts of data for credit assessment (Liang et al., 2018).
Moreover, the SCSs have established a joint reward‐punishment mechanism offering
incentives for good behaviors and sanctions for trust‐breaking behaviors (Creemers, 2018;
Engelmann et al., 2019). This mechanism has the potential to discipline individual behavior
and foster law‐abiding citizens (Kostka & Antoine, 2019). Although Western media and
observers have widely criticized the SCSs, research suggests that Chinese citizens show a
high level of approval of the project (Kostka, 2019). More recently, scholars consider China's
SCSs as a speciﬁc instance of the global expansion of quantiﬁcation practices (Bach, 2020;
Mac Síthigh & Siems, 2019). The rise of the SCSs hence indicates governments’ schema to
develop infrastructures for quantifying and monitoring people.
     One of the most important aspects of the SCSs is the proﬁling of individuals. Currently,
China is building several parallel systems for rating personal reputation and creditworthiness
(Liu, 2019). The multiplicity further complicates the understanding of the SCSs since these
systems have different objectives and rely on distinct databases. First, the People's Bank of
China (PBC) has developed the Credit Reference Center since 2004, aiming to evaluate
lending and economic activities of individuals and corporations using information from
mortgages, credit cards, delayed payments, and law enforcement (Liu, 2019). Second,
China's commercial credit rating agencies have rapidly expanded since 2015 (Chong, 2019).
Eight companies including Ant Financial and Tencent are encouraged to build digital
platforms for assessing customer credit. In addition to ﬁnancial data, these platforms also
collect people's online networks and interactions (Kostka, 2019). In 2018, PBC launched
Baihang Credit with these companies and National Internet Finance Association of China,
seeking to share credit data and promote credit assessment.
     However, while the body of work on national SCSs is burgeoning, little attention has
been paid to the evaluation of personal social credit at local levels. In fact, China's municipal
governments are actively implementing the SCSs for assessing the creditworthiness of
citizens. The earliest case is Suining's public credit management system launched in 2010.
Since 2015, multiple cities have been involved in building local SCSs (Mac Síthigh &
Siems, 2019). These local schemes emphasize social and economic life and calculate
numeric scores for residents (Liu, 2019). Nevertheless, we know very little about how local
SCSs collect data and rate personal credit, and what indicators have been adopted in the

SCSs. Therefore, the current scholarship needs to interrogate the implementation and
operation of the SCSs in pilot cities. In this study, we address the following research
questions:
   RQ1: What personal data have been collected by the local SCSs to render what types of
knowledge about citizens visible?
   RQ2: How do the technical and institutional features of the SCSs shape how

   RQ3: What are the potential ramiﬁcations of employing the SCSs as the infrastructure of
social quantiﬁcation?


ME THODS

Data
This study relies on policy documentation, ofﬁcial regulations and platforms, news coverage,
and gray literature to map out the technological, political, and social dimensions of the
SCSs. First, we collected current policies regarding personal social credit issued by the
central government and 32 provincial‐level governments. Next, we compiled a list of China's
prefecture‐level cities, as local SCSs have been mainly operated in these cities. We then
performed keyword searches using city names and personal social credit on Credit China
and local credit platforms (e.g., Credit Hangzhou). This yielded 247 documents: 158 are
general plans for the construction of the SCSs and 89 involve the design of the SCSs. We
paid particular attention to these 89 documents to understand how the SCSs are designed
and operated in prefecture‐level cities. Finally, we monitored public discussion about the
SCSs by gathering news articles, academic and gray literature published between January
2015 and June 2021. These sources supplement information and help us gain further
understanding of the potential implications of the SCSs.


Analysis
We draw on a policy implementation method to examine how the SCSs are designed and
implemented, and what are the possible consequences. As a policy analysis method, policy
implementation explores how governmental agencies put policies into action and what
factors explain the effectiveness of implementations (Nilsen et al., 2013). This approach
allows researchers to systematically analyze state‐sponsored infrastructures, since these
infrastructures are mainly designed by public policies (Freeman et al., 2019). In this study,
we consider the SCSs as a centrally‐coordinated policy process by which municipal
governments need to follow the chain of command from central policy‐makers and
meantime design and carry out local experiments. Previous literature suggests that the
Chinese central government encourages local ofﬁcials to simultaneously test various
versions of policies (Heilmann, 2008). Hence, we adopt a “top‐down” approach viewing
policy implementation as a decision‐making pipeline from policymakers to local experi-
mentation (Nilsen et al., 2013). This allows us to comprehensively investigate the
implementation and operation of the SCSs at local levels.
    We began our data analysis by closely reading national and provincial policies. While
these policies did not provide details about local systems, they helped us understand the
top‐down mechanisms of the SCSs. Next we turned to municipal policies. We ﬁrst read
policies about quantiﬁcation practices, paying close attention to the processes and
indicators of calculating credit scores. This allowed us to explore the modular designs

and components of the SCSs. We also considered the indicators used for credit assessment
as a way to reverse‐engineer political logic embedded in the SCSs, as they imply how
political goals are imposed into social quantiﬁcation to deﬁne what might be a good citizen.
We thus identiﬁed these indicators mentioned in our data. Finally, we analyzed how ofﬁcial
policies and news media discussed the potential implications of the SCSs, particularly the
reward and punishment mechanisms in these cities.


RESULTS

Our analysis shows that the SCSs are portrayed in a particular narrative about credit
crises and technological solutions. Both national and municipal documentations claim
that the decline of trustworthiness and morals can be solved via data and technologies.
This vision indicates China's attempt to leverage digital technologies for addressing
social and economic challenges. Currently, Chinese prefecture‐level cities are at
different stages of implementing the SCSs. While some have launched the SCSs for
assessing citizens, most cities are still in the design and planning stage. First, we ﬁnd
that 41 cities have launched the SCSs since 2015. Figure 1 illustrates that launched
SCSs are predominantly located in coastal provinces including Jiangsu, Zhejiang, and
Shandong. One possible explanation is that these cities have larger populations
and better economic performance. Two exceptions are Guiyang in Guizhou province
and Baoji in Shaanxi province. Second, we ﬁnd that 38 pilot cities are expected to
operate the SCSs in the near future, so we label them as forthcoming SCSs. Cities in
this group geographically expand to the Middle and Western regions. Altogether,
launched and forthcoming SCSs covers over 400 million populations.
    Finally, 98 cities have announced plans for building local SCSs. We thus label them
as SCSs under planning. Moreover, 156 cities do not publish policies for building the
SCSs. This does not mean that they will not implement the SCSs, since almost all

Governments as data integrators and processors

This section addresses RQ1 by focusing on 50 local SCSs potentially covering 210 million
population. Social quantiﬁcation often involves special types of modularity consisting of a set
of components and designs. These structures constitute the properties of a technological
system and often present the ﬁxed materiality (Leonardi, 2012). We conceptualize
technological structures of the SCSs as the software, standards, and protocols that enable
the collection, measurement, and analysis of personal data pertaining to social credit. Our
analysis shows that local SCSs in these 50 cities ﬁt different ideas and initiatives into a
standardized protocol for rating and ranking citizens. It thus crowds out alternative visions
and explanations of assessing “good” citizens. Such approaches allow local governments to
calculate and compare citizens in real‐time.
    The ﬁrst step of local SCSs is the aggregation of data warehouses involving the
deployment of new infrastructures capable of gathering data from multiple sources. We ﬁnd
that the SCSs gather two types of data: identity and behavior data. First, identity data are
used to target the status of individuals, often including ten variables (name, gender, ID
number, date of birth, education, degree, Hukou, home address, marital status, and career
qualiﬁcation). Identity data are standardized in these local systems. Second, behavioral data
are measurements of what people do, involving positive and negative behaviors collected
from multiple ofﬁcial sources. This type of data will be updated every month and thus
enables real‐time evaluations.
    Contrary to the widespread expectation that the SCSs extract digital tracing data, we ﬁnd
that local SCSs rely heavily on pre‐existing administrative databases. In fact, all identity and
behavioral data are ofﬁcial records already collected by governments and state‐owned
enterprises in their routine activities. Thus, the SCSs contain the pre‐established
technologies and institutions of Hukou‐based authentication of citizens. Moreover, we also
ﬁnd that public security departments play a key role in aggregating behavioral data. In Yiwu
and Fuzhou, for instance, public security bureaus contribute to more than 10% of behavioral
data. Other important agencies include local courts, justice bureaus, and urban
administrative and law enforcement bureaus.
    The second step is the transformation of heterogeneous qualities into a common
metric. Our analysis shows that the SCSs employ an assemblage of heterogeneous
indicators to transform qualitative differences into quantiﬁable metrics for calculating
how many points should be granted to a citizen. Speciﬁcally, the SCSs often assign a
basic score to each citizen and then assess their behavioral data. We ﬁnd that the basic
score reﬂects local customization: 17 cities assign 1000 points for residents, whereas
others choose 860, 500, or 100 as their basic scores (Table A1). Furthermore, these
cities adopt distinct scales for credit scores. Twenty‐one cities enact a maximum point,
so citizens cannot obtain a score higher than certain points. For example, credit scores
in Suzhou, Zhengzhou, and Yiwu's range from 0 to 200, while personal scores in
Quzhou and Nanyang are from 0 to 1000. By contrast, other cities do not set a
maximum score. It is also worth noting that currently local SCSs are separate from the
8    |                                                                             LIANG AND CHEN



central government platforms like Credit China. While these pilot cities can report trust‐
breaking behaviors to Credit China, most of their records are saved in local databases.
    Behavioral data function as indicators to determine whether people earn or lose points.
Indicators and scores hence express the degree to which people's past activities statistically
relate to their creditworthiness. However, these cities do not provide details about statistical
models and methods used in their systems. Our analysis suggests the SCSs do not involve
advanced technology like Artiﬁcial Intelligence, instead the technological structures enable a
simple scoring mechanism. Basically, a social credit score is calculated by summing the
points received by each individual. Importantly, we ﬁnd that there is no agreed‐upon version
of what indicators constitute social credit scores among these systems. While some cities
such as Ruzhou employ more than 200 predictors in their systems, Jiangyin and Puyang
only have 37 and 28 indicators, respectively.
    In the ﬁnal step, numerical scores are converted into ordinal levels of measurement,
aiming to further categorize and rank citizens. We ﬁnd that 43 cities have released their
credit rankings and two types of labels are used to label these rankings: 25 cities use
alphabetically‐labeled ordinal measurement ranging from AAA to D, while others introduce
name‐labeled measurement (i.e., Excellent, Good, Fair, Poor, Risky). These cities also use
distinct categories to differentiate personal social credit: nine cities employ eight categories,
14 have six categories, and 17 adopt ﬁve categories. Additionally, they have set cut‐off
points to distinguish the level of social credit, but the decision‐making of the thresholds is
also inconsistent. For instance, Luohe and Yulin use the same basic score (1000) and
categories (six) but different cut‐off points. While a person with 1100 points in Luohe is
classiﬁed as AAA citizen, she is considered as A citizen in Yulin. People can review their
credit scores and rankings via ofﬁcial apps, allowing them to learn details about social credit
and know how to improve their credit (Figure 2).
    Overall, the standardized protocol means that the SCSs are designed by the top
leadership and experimented by the local governments with the intention to transform
multiple dimensions of personal life into quantiﬁable metrics, and meanwhile, speciﬁc
indicators and scoring mechanisms are substantially ﬂexible. In fact, the underlying
structures of the local SCSs indicate infrastructure's simultaneous needs for common
standards and tailorable indicators (Star & Ruhleder, 1996). It also echoes the claim that
China's central policy‐makers encourage various local experimentations for public policies
(Heilmann, 2008). Moreover, the SCSs build on pre‐existing ofﬁcial records, showing how
political institutions and structures are always already part of the social quantiﬁcation
infrastructures. Taken together, quantiﬁcation practices tightly interweave with social
contexts and existing power relations, rendering citizens visible as data to the state.

The calculative and measuring practices of the SCSs
We further explore metrics and calculative practices to understand how the technical and
bureaucratic features of the SCSs shape how governments assess citizens (RQ2). We
argue that these metrics are the most important part of social quantiﬁcation, as they show
the rationales of what is a “good” citizen to the state. Here metrics indicate the credit
indicators and credit‐granting process in local SCSs, and they inscribe inherently political
values and institutional mechanisms for calculating and comparing citizens. By analyzing
multiple metrics and scoring mechanisms, we can reverse‐engineer what and how political
goals are imposed into the technological structures of the SCSs to determine what might be
a good citizen.
     People can boost their credit scores by engaging with three types of activities: awards,
social charity, and others (Table 1). Speciﬁcally, the award category contains ofﬁcial awards
and commendations, including good citizen awards, merit awards, entrepreneurship
awards, and science and technology awards. Citizens can earn certain points (e.g., 10,
30, 100) if they receive these awards. Furthermore, social charities include volunteer
activities, acts of heroism and courage, returning found money, benevolent activities, blood
donations, and even body bequest. For instance, people in Suifenhe can receive ﬁve points
for blood donations and 5–50 points for charitable donations. Finally, several cities also
consider other trust‐keeping activities like wildlife rescue (Yiwu) and providing hints for
public security departments (Fuzhou). We ﬁnd that the points for the same indicator often
vary in these cities. For example, people in Weihai can receive 150 points if they have
provincial‐level moral rewards, but citizens in Nanyang only get 60 points for the same
rewards.
     Table 1 illustrates that around 45% of indicators for points accumulation are related to
awards and 36% are from social charities, suggesting that the SCSs predominantly consider
social morality and moral actions as trust‐keeping behaviors for earning points. This also
means that those who receive ofﬁcial awards and actively take part in social charities are
regarded as “good” citizens by local SCSs. The points accumulation can be used to
encourage social morality and raise sincerity culture in these cities. Thus, local SCSs are
signiﬁcantly different from customer credit rating in China, because social morality and
sincerity are important factors affecting credit scores.
     By contrast, citizens will lose points if they have records in the following categories:
social management, commercial defaults, judicial judgments, government affairs, and
others. First, social management involves various activities including trafﬁc violations,
jaywalking, cheating on tests, lack of care for the elderly, administrative penalties, and being
in arrears on water bills, gas bills, and cellphone bills. For example, people in Suqian will
lose 20–160 points if they fail to pay electricity bills, and people in Jiangyin may lose 50–200
points if they have trafﬁc violations. Again, the number of indicators varies among these
citizens: Rongcheng has 176 indicators to measure social management, Shangqiu only has
seven indicators. Overall, this category accounts for 42% of negative behaviors, making it
the most important category for points deduction.
     Another notable ﬁnding is that social management involves broader contexts.
Rongcheng, for instance, includes Xinfang (petitioning) in its system. Local residents will
lose 10–50 points if they participate in 17 Xinfang‐related activities, including petitioning in
Beijing, staying at Xinfang agencies, and repeated complaints using online Xinfang system.
Meanwhile, people in Rongcheng will lose 20–50 points if they spread misinformation on
digital media. Similarly, Suifenhe, Wulian, Erenhot, and Ruzhou also regard the distribution
of misinformation on WeChat as an indicator for points deduction. The concentration on
social management indicates that the SCSs go beyond traditional credit reporting and thus
should be considered as a social management system rather than ﬁnancial credit agencies.

     Next, commercial defaults include activities like being in arrears on bank loans or
housing loans, underpayment of tax, and insurance fraud. These activities are associated
with traditional ﬁnancial credit, indicating that the SCSs also monitor ﬁnancial and
commercial life. We ﬁnd that around 15% of trust‐breaking behaviors are based on
commercial defaults. Finally, judicial judgments contain information about law enforcement,
civil litigations, and criminal proceedings, while government affairs focus on civil servants.
Many cities also include other information like the blacklist for credit assessment. Obviously,
the majority of trust‐breaking behaviors are concerned with violations of laws and
regulations, rather than economic and lending activities. For example, Erenhot publishes
information about points deduction every month. Among 197 cases released online between
October and December 2019, 115 were related to administrative detentions or penalties, 68
were due to trafﬁc violations, and the remaining 14 were also from social management.
Therefore, the SCSs largely expand the scope of credit assessment by focusing on social
management and law enforcement.
     Moreover, local systems differ greatly in terms of speciﬁc indicators and scoring
mechanisms. For instance, while Weihai employs more than 2800 items to calculate
personal social credit, cities like Nanyang and Yulin have less than 30 indicators (Table 1).
The maximum points a person can earn or lose also vary. Nanyang and Ordos adopt the
same basic score and rankings, but citizens in Nanyang can earn a maximum of 590 points
from 8 indicators whilst people in Ordos can gain a maximum of 338 points from 15 items.
Interestingly, we also ﬁnd that these cities employ more indicators for points deduction than
points accumulation. While the average indicators for raising credit scores are 51 in these
cities, that number increases to 256 for losing points. This further suggests that local
governments imagine the SCSs as a tool for restricting inadequate behaviors and improving
social management.
     Taken together, the measuring and calculative practices imbued with the SCSs indicate
the built‐in aspirations of what might be “good” citizens. Our result shows that the SCSs
contain multiple dimensions including social morality, ﬁnancial credit, legal enforcement and
compliance, and law‐abiding actions. These local experimentations consider social morality
as trust‐keeping behaviors and desired outcomes, encouraging citizens to actively
participate in moral thinking and actions. Meanwhile, trust‐breaking behaviors are mainly
related to social management, meaning that local governments attempt to achieve greater
levels of social manipulation and law enforcement. These aspirations are enabled by
embedding quantiﬁcation practices in everyday life, allowing the state power to
comprehensively render citizens visible as data and intervene in social and economic life.
Consequently, the SCSs normalize the notion of “good” citizens and ultimately induce moral
and law‐abiding behaviors, while citizens are expected to comply with moral norms and
entrenched power hierarchies.


Discriminatory services and behavior modiﬁcation for deviance
It is too early to evaluate the effectiveness of the SCSs, because these systems have only
been established in the past few years. In this section, we examine the potential implications
of these local schemes by looking at the reward and punishment mechanism (RQ3). As
mentioned, credit rankings enable the SCSs to sort people with different scores into
homogeneous categories, thereby simplifying social and political complexities. In this
process, local SCSs deﬁne what is good and what is deviant, whilst people are constantly
measured against one another. We ﬁnd that these cities employ standardized categories for
reward and punishment. In particular, rewards emphasize incentives in public services,
POLICY & INTERNET                                                                         |   13



ﬁnance, administration, and public praise, whereas punishments include restrictions in
administration, consumption, ﬁnance, public shaming, and other areas (Table A2).
    Citizens in A/Good category will receive various rewards and beneﬁts to lower
transaction costs and obtain priority. To be speciﬁc, around 44% of rewards are related to
public services. Eligible citizens can receive, for instance, discounts for parking, public
transportation, and public gyms. They can also get deposit waivers in libraries and hospitals,
fast‐tracks in schools, hospitals, and other public institutions. Cities like Rongcheng also
offer healthcare beneﬁts and social welfare for AA and AAA citizens. Moreover, 28% of
rewards are ﬁnancial and economic beneﬁts. A/Good citizens can get lower interest rates
and tax reductions, access government procurement, and obtain ﬁnancial support. Further,
administrative rewards include priority for administrative approval and political promotions,
accounting for 13% of rewards. This type of beneﬁt is only granted to civil servants and party
members. Citizens in A/Good group are more likely to be promoted by local governments.
Finally, AAA/Excellent citizens in 12 cities also receive public praise, such as news coverage
and local redlist.
    By contrast, citizens in C/Poor category would receive multiple sanctions aiming to
revoke eligibility and set restrictions. First, 38% of punishments are related to administrative
sanctions. For instance, citizens in C and D groups cannot obtain awards and
commendations from local governments, and they are banned from taking up employment
in the governments and public institutions. Meanwhile, 27% of punishment seeks to limit
people's access to economic activities including commercial insurance and the increase of
interest rates. C‐level citizens need to pay higher interest rates and are restricted from
certain ﬁnancial services. Moreover, some cities also constrain consumption for D citizens.
People in this category cannot buy ﬂights and high‐speed train tickets, as well as luxury
vehicles and apartments. Their kids are also restricted from attending private schools.
Finally, public shaming is widely used to punish those who have low credit scores. Local
governments can publish trust‐breaking information on local media.
    Obviously, the SCSs can deliver discriminatory services using credit rankings. People
with higher scores and rankings are considered as “good” citizens and thus receive material
beneﬁts and reputational gains. Conversely, those who have lower rankings are classiﬁed
as “bad” citizens and hence get multiple restrictions and exclusions. Thus, the SCSs
generate distinctions between seemingly monolithic citizens and subsequently allocates
different beneﬁts and sanctions. More remarkably, the scoring mechanisms and
discriminatory services allow the SCSs to practically steer and modify individual behavior.
Previous literature suggests that people tend to change their behaviors in reaction to being
evaluated or measured (Espeland & Sauder, 2007). Splitting and sorting citizens into
categories potentially shape social life and redistribute resources, thereby disciplining
individual behavior towards certain desirable goals deﬁned by the governments. Our result
suggests that the SCSs have been designed to modify individual behavior through two
approaches: point accumulation and credit repair. While the former encourages citizens to
engage with socially accepted actions, the latter requires citizens to express their
commitments to the government.
    As discussed, the political logic of the SCSs is that local governments attempt to
encourage social morality and enforce social management. This goal is further supported by
the reward and punishment mechanisms imposed on the SCSs. To receive beneﬁts and
avoid punishments, citizens are motivated to participate in activities that can raise their
credit scores. In fact, the SCSs explicitly encourage people to get extra points by engaging
in charitable donations, volunteer services, blood donations, and other social charitable
activities, which are often not necessarily trustworthy or creditworthy related behaviors.
Citizens are also expected to exercise self‐discipline for maximizing their credit scores and
14   |                                                                            LIANG AND CHEN



be in conformity with the norms. Therefore, the point accumulation could stimulate people's
engagement with social charities and law‐abiding actions.
     In addition, people can improve their scores and rankings by signing credit commitment
letters, promising that they will not take part in trust‐breaking actions. The credit commitment
letter also asks people to assure that they will participate in law‐abiding activities and
strengthen self‐discipline. People in some cities need to attend credit education and training
to receive credit repair. Further, several cities publicly announce that people cannot repair
their credit if they intentionally take part in trust‐breaking behaviors, their actions damage
social orders and the government's credibility, or they disrupt online information ﬂows. Credit
repair shows that people are expected to be responsible for monitoring and self‐discipline
their behaviors and show commitments to governments. As such, the SCSs are socially
transformative, since it plays a key role in reshaping and reinforcing social orders and state‐
citizen relations.
     However, the effectiveness of the SCSs still remains in question. While the system can
encourage self‐discipline and self‐restraint, they may not be able to enforce internalizing
behaviors. This raises questions about the intrinsic quality of citizens, as people are mainly
motivated by external regulations and incentives to enact trust‐keeping activities. We ﬁnd
cities like Hangzhou aim to commodify credit scores so citizens can obtain more material
beneﬁts. Thus, credit scores do not necessarily mean increased morality and sincerity.


DISCUSSION
This article examines the technological structures, political logic, and potential implications
of China's local SCSs. While neither social quantiﬁcation nor real‐time monitoring in
administrative decision‐making is a novel phenomenon, the SCSs introduce a new way of
deepening dataﬁcation in identifying and classifying people. We reveal that the SCSs enable
standardized processes for quantifying personal life, whereas potential metrics and scoring
mechanisms are substantially ﬂexible at local levels. In particular, the SCSs make people
legible and thus reconstruct them into data subjects that can be acted upon, in our case,
being rated, categorized, and sanctioned or rewarded. Moreover, these local systems
attempt to separate and exclude people so that they can deﬁne “good” citizens and
normalize “deviant” behaviors. Consequently, the SCSs shift state‐citizen relations in
substantively important ways by tightly mingling quantiﬁcation practices with social and
economic life.
    We argue that local SCSs function as infrastructures of social quantiﬁcation to split and
sort people into classiﬁcatory schemes for simplifying social contexts and normalizing social
consensus. The SCSs encompass both technological architectures and political values of
moral judgements deﬁned by the governments of what is trustworthy, requiring state power
or other actors for design, deployment, operation, and evolution. The fundamental purpose
of local SCSs, hence, is not only to render people visible but to create a reality of such
governance and social consensus of “creditworthiness” and “good” through continuous
rating and ranking practices enabled by digital technologies and data. This allows state
power to see and categorize citizens in an increasingly abstract and calculable way, thereby
generating discriminatory proﬁling and modifying individual behavior.
    We further propose a framework to examine the SCSs from three overarching
dimensions: technological structures, political logic, and potential implications. The
framework systematically explores how the social quantiﬁcation infrastructures like the
SCSs work, who control them, and what they can do. The ﬁrst layer technological structures
refer to the architectures, components, structural designs, and decision‐making pipelines
that support the implementation and operation of sociotechnical systems. Technological
POLICY & INTERNET                                                                                             |   15



structures include software and hardware components conﬁgured to constitute certain
infrastructures. These structures constitute the properties of a technological system and are
available for all users (Leonardi, 2012). Hence, a comprehensive analysis of technological
structures helps understand how social quantiﬁcation is deployed and operated in the
background.
     The second layer is political logic entrenched into technological structures. Admittedly,
social quantiﬁcation infrastructure has inherently embedded in existing social and political
values and institutional structures in the design, implementation, and operation (Star &
Ruhleder, 1996). Thus, we need to investigate the entrenched power relations and political
purposes underlying technological structures of social quantiﬁcation. Finally, all social
quantiﬁcation infrastructures have potential goals that reshape and reconstruct social orders
and power relations (Espeland & Sauder, 2007). While infrastructures help state power
achieve political goals through certain technological structures, they also generate
boundaries for excluding people and regulating individual behavior. Therefore, it is vital to
investigate how these boundaries facilitate new ways of seeing people and reshaping state‐
citizen relations.
     In this study, we do not analyze how citizens make sense of and react to the SCSs
because local SCSs are novel phenomena and people may not fully perceive their functions.
While local SCSs have the potential to facilitate “good” citizens and discipline “deviant”
behaviors, it is unclear whether such quantiﬁcation will be sufﬁcient to achieve the goal. It is
possible that the SCSs may not internalize people's behaviors, thereby limiting the ability to
enhance sincerity culture and enforce law‐abiding behaviors. Therefore, future research
should examine the effectiveness of local SCSs by focusing on how people understand and
respond to the SCSs. Another limitation of this study is that we do not investigate how SCSs
are related to credit scoring on digital platforms. As mentioned, emerging SCSs are parallel
to the rise of quantiﬁcation on Chinese platforms like Alipay. It is hence interesting to
examine how ofﬁcial quantiﬁcation is connected to and different from commercial
quantiﬁcation.
     To conclude, China's SCSs represent an important shift in the intervention of
quantiﬁcation technologies into everyday life. In this article, we underscore that the SCSs
have been extensively adopted by local governments for assessing personal social credit
and regulating individual behavior. At present, China has no uniﬁed SCSs and more cities
are expected to launch their systems in the next few years. We have shown that the SCSs
might enable state power to intervene in social and economic life by rating and ranking
citizens. The rise of SCSs thus indicates China's ambitions to raise social morality and
upgrade social management using digital technologies and massive datasets.
