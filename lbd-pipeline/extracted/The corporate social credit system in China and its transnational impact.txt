    ABSTRACT
    The Social Credit System (SCS) is on the verge of becoming a new regulatory
    reality. The CCP is changing its governance tools by combining social
    management and big data. This article provides an overview on the possible
    impact of the SCS on corporations (CSCS) and analyses its potential
    extraterritorial eﬀects. The role of transnational law (TL) as a political project is
    discussed and TL is thus a subject of this paper. Yet, concepts of TL are also used
    methodologically, as an analytical framework, to make subtle extraterritorial eﬀect
    and potential legal change visible. The paper places the CSCS in the centre of the
    analysis and considers technological development, a changing geopolitical
    landscape, economic interdependence and the behaviour of individuals. The
    CSCS is an emerging example of the strategic and ﬂexible norm diﬀusion of
    powerful states—which underlines the importance of TL as a problem orientated
    and border-transcending analytical framework.

interdependence networks



1. The CSCS—where technological development, national
regulation and transnational impact meet
It is no news that the Chinese Communist Party (CCP)1 aspires to gain
inﬂuence within the international system. The Belt and Road Initiative is
by now a well-known example. Yet, this article aims to describe how the
People’s Republic of China (PRC) could gain inﬂuence in international
corporate regulation by using algorithmic governance, the Social Credit
System (SCS), as well as the global economy and interdependence
hubs. New technologies, a momentum of political change, the mechanics
of globalisation as well as the growing international weight of China

1.1. Methodology and limitations
The analysis of the CSCS, or the ‘problem’ as Jessup would call it,17 warrants
a method which retraces the movement of law across the distinction of the
private and the public, and makes the political character of the private
visible as well as the potential political usage of it.18 Transnational law
looks beyond the dichotomies of the domestic and international,19 as well
as the individual and the state—just as the problem at hand does not stop
at the borders of these categories as it will be seen below. As the ‘[b]oundaries
between distinctly perceived legal and political, jurisdictionally and system-
atically perceived systems begin to blur’20 transnational law focuses on the
inner connections and similarities among allegedly diﬀerent problems,
across collapsed boundaries between the domestic and the international.21
What this means for the CSCS and the transnational as a subject of the analy-
sis will be presented in the following chapters.
    It has to be noted that this paper presents an external view on the topic,
which may diﬀer from an internal one. Many Chinese citizens welcome the
SCS. This approval is mainly shaped by the promise of improved quality of
life, increased government oversight, accountability in business aﬀairs and
the closure of regulatory gaps.22 The main incentive for the SCS is not to
extend Chinese global inﬂuence. The drivers of the system are internal

for private individuals beyond China, it is actually a wider concept that
encompasses the regulation of society and state in general. The SCS is not
(yet) a single uniﬁed and nation-wide system. The current reality of the
SCS is heterogenous and scattered across hundreds of regulations and thou-

purposes; or to connect transnational law with ideology and interests.
Especially during colonial times concepts such as ‘civilization’ or ‘civilized

entering this transnational sphere as well as national regulation. The states
and institutions which had the main economic impact, were the main pro-

as national acceptance and to use international law for their stabilisation.43

lated by international norms that are negotiated in the inter-state fora
between states44 and to a considerable extent by soft law and regulations
that are rather private regulatory norms designed by the market actors them-

political economy—law that is nurtured by state and non-state actors, com-
prising national, international and transnational sources, using hard and soft
law—works to the advantage of private actors and makes transnational law
operate as the ‘common sense’ of contemporary global capitalism.46
   However, the content of the political project does not have to remain
unchanged or unchallenged. Sentiments of global crisis and instability desta-
bilise the authority of ideas and actors that contribute to the (current)
common sense. ‘[C]risis creates uncertainty that destabilizes background
assumptions and perceptions of interest regarding legal and institutional
change’.47 It is thus a favourable moment for challenging the status quo
and introducing new rules in which a diﬀerent common sense is embedded.

This is even more so today, due to global interdependencies and technological

   Transnational law used as a method can oﬀer an analytical framework to
the CSCS as it follows the movement of law across the domains of the
public and the private, the national and the international, the economic
and the political. It is diﬃcult to assess the SCS along the contours of
public and private as the system ‘encourages the ﬂow of big data on indi-
viduals amongst public authorities and private entities’.50 Governance in
China has had a broad scope for centuries, encompassing the aim to
ensure the morality of the individual, the society and the state. The law
is not seen as an autonomous sphere and the boundary between the
public and private is blurred, the categories of the legal and the moral inter-
twined.51 The SCS reﬂects this ‘holistic’ perception of law and is supposed
to break geographical and jurisdictional barriers.52 The CSCS cannot be
separated entirely from the SCS which evaluates individual behaviour
and thus this problem goes beyond transnational corporate regulation
and geopolitics and includes a human aspect. Transnational law takes the
human aspect into account and helps to ‘embrace a wider and more
adequate view of global human activities’.53

to monitor, regulate and control economic activity on its territory—also to
the beneﬁt of the actors for example by combatting fraud and product
piracy.54 This national origin does not contradict the conception of transna-
tional law in this paper. The transnational is not a physical space above the
national but a construct linked to the national: ‘[G]lobalization results in the

3. The CSCS and its potential impact on corporations and
transnational corporate governance and international relations
Data-driven governance is not a Chinese invention and neither are its pro-
blematic aspects59 but the SCS, as envisioned, in its almost all-encompassing
scale is unprecedented.60 While the use of social media data and other data
points can be positive for some, for example when the lack of ﬁnancial
history has previously been a hindrance for credit,61 it can be a curse for
others as the use of correlation of diﬀerent data sets instead of causation
can lead to discriminatory practices.62 Many algorithmic assessment tools
operate with ﬂawed proxies that establish harmful correlations in order to
predict behaviour, trustworthiness and risk and thereby infringe rights.63

3.2. The CSCS as a technology-governance-hub and as a driver for
practical change
The CSCS has direct consequences for corporations and their employees and
as a result it inﬂuences interests and values fostered in global corporate regu-
lation. Additionally, it could also be used as a governance tool within the
realm of international relations, be it for sanctions and co-optation or econ-
omic coercion. In their research, Farrell and Newman focus on asymmetric
network structures that create interdependence but not to the beneﬁt of all
but rather they ‘create the potential for “weaponized interdependence” in
which network topography generates enduring power imbalances’.106 The
CSCS, the institutions regulating it and the corporations providing data
and technical assistance for the assessment are located in the PRC and this
could create a new ‘hub’ in the global network structure if the rating
system becomes a requirement for economic interaction. Hubs, even when
they serve a global network, are controlled locally by the states in which
the companies and institutions of that hub are physically present. Recently,
a transition has taken place in China from many medium-size companies to
large dominant players in the Chinese Internet environment. The monopoly
that corporations such as Baidu, Alibaba and Tencent possess, results in the
ability of the state to regulate the majority of online activities if it controls
these companies107 or:

      omic and information ﬂows, discover and exploit vulnerabilities, compel
      policy change, and deter unwanted actions.108

claim of power in the global political economy.


4. Conclusion
This analysis has shown that the CSCS is border transcending in many ways.
Its characteristics amplify the intertwinement of legal categories and empha-
sise the factor of the individual in corporate decision-making which makes
this national regulatory tool a transnational legal problem. It is a transna-
tional legal problem that exempliﬁes the political aspects of transnational
law and as such it is a subject of this paper. Currently, the transnational pol-
itical project is working towards corporate and capitalist interests; it is
described as creating a common sense within the global political economy.

Yet, this current common sense is not immune to change. The CSCS can be
used to control discourse, inﬂuence knowledge structures and enhance
claims to right and ultimately power.
   The possible impact of the CSCS on individuals and corporations is con-
nected to its highly ﬂexible and partially opaque nature. The various ways
that allow the social management of individuals and entities, its sources
which can be political as well as non-political, the vagueness of some
terms as well as the informal character of some features stand in contrast
to the legal possibilities to respond to them. This can foster anticipatory obe-
dience—or, put diﬀerently, a change in corporate culture and the common
sense of corporate regulation.
   Chapter II has shown that the CSCS can result in an extraterritorial eﬀect
and in the ﬂow of norms as it inﬂuences corporations to act according to a
political project that is linked to CCP’s version of state-capitalism. Since cor-
porations operate transnationally there is a chance that they transport this
political project via individuals, business partners, supply chains or custo-
mers across the PRC’s borders. The mechanisms behind the CSCS re-
create an old play on a slightly altered scene. The topic of the play is well-
known. Internally, it is the maintenance or re-centralisation of the power
of the CCP and the use of ‘ﬂexible’ and ‘moralistic’ approaches to governance
as opposed to a ‘stiﬀ’ legalistic method.129 This internal development has a
transnational impact. If the PRC becomes a leading ‘distributor’ of knowl-
edge on algorithmic regulation, a central node for transnational data and
the location of a hub for corporate governance, this is a source of
inﬂuence. It is not a new development that the PRC uses interdependence
structures to shape the (political) behaviour of corporations—neither is
the PRC the only country to do so.130 Yet, the tools with which this game
is played change. The CSCS thus becomes an enforcement tool for the
CCP’s political aims in its international relations.
   Hence, the CSCS is not just an example for corporate regulation on the
basis of big data or a technological value-neutral solution for enhancing abi-
dance to the law but highlights the fact that regulation is political and that the
private is politicised in transnational law. Algorithms function according to
men-made, or state-oﬃcial-made, and interest-based models, which com-
prise values, ideology and interests. With the CSCS and, thus, through the
corporate door and state-run algorithms, the CCP might be able to
set alternative standards for international economic and business regulation,
gain control over discourse and alter the common sense of transnational cor-
porate regulation to its advantage and to stabilise or enhance its power in the
international sphere.

    Next to transnational law as a political project, this example also shows
that transnational law as a method can serve as a valuable framework for
the analysis of the CSCS as a transnational legal problem. Concepts of trans-
national law, such as symbolic and practical change, follow the ‘movement’
of law across the legal distinction of public and private and serve as a lens
through which the mechanism of the system and its transnational impact
are made visible. It shows how a national system can have transnational
eﬀects, not only in terms of adoption of a foreign law within a certain legis-
lation, but potentially by eﬀecting small and gradual changes in individual
and corporate behaviour, thus practical change. This change happens on
the level of individuals and their aspirations, identities, fears and societal
and economic needs. This highlights the importance of the human factor
when analysing such regulation, which is crucial in the context of competing
governance systems that make gradual practical change more likely than
symbolic change.
    This article touched upon transnational law as a political project and the
possibility that the CSCS alters the content of this political project to the
beneﬁt of the Chinese government. The private is politicised but it
remains to be seen into which direction the common sense of transnational
corporate regulation will ultimately develop. The article also used transna-
tional law as a method to trace the construction and ﬂow of norms in the
context of geopolitical as well as technological change: New regulatory
tools can be based on new technologies, as it is the case with the CSCS.
This demonstrates the symbiotic relationship between regulation, techno-
logical development and the maintenance, enhancement or loss of transna-
tional inﬂuence. This strategic and ﬂexible norm diﬀusion of powerful states,
which the CSCS might be an emerging example of, underlines the relevance
of transnational law as a problem-oriented and border-transcending analyti-
cal framework.
